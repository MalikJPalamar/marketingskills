### CENTAURION.ME — WHAT WE DO

**The problem we solve:**

Most AI implementations are thermostats. They respond to inputs, produce outputs, and stop. They don't learn from their mistakes. They don't compound over time. They do not become more accurate or more capable with use.

This is an architectural problem. It is not solved by better models, more data, or more AI hires. It is solved by building a closed-loop system — where prediction, measurement, error, and feedback are designed into the architecture from the beginning.

**What we build:**

Centaurion.me helps mid-market organizations architect closed-loop human-AI augmentation systems. We use a structured framework — the Centaurion Method — to diagnose, design, and build AI infrastructure that compounds over time.

**Three Laws. One Equation. Five Sensing Layers.**

The Centaurion Method is built on:
- **Closed-Loop Primacy:** Every AI system must measure its prediction errors and feed them back into the model
- **Human Sensing Superiority:** Humans must be positioned at the points of highest contextual sensing value
- **Compounding by Design:** AI systems that don't improve with use degrade relative to systems that do

The Fitness Equation — Sensing × Reasoning × Feedback / Implementation Complexity — provides an organizational AI health metric that we measure, track, and improve over time.

**Our IP:**

The 11 Levels of Agentic Engineering is an open-source framework (GitHub: MalikJPalamar/centaurion-agentic-engineering-lvl) that defines the 11 capability states organizations move through as they develop agentic AI infrastructure. Most organizations in 2026 are at Levels 2–4. The assessment takes 5 minutes and identifies the highest-value gaps.

### Diagnostic Questions (Minutes 5–20)

Ask 4–6 of these. Don't rush. Let them answer fully. The best discovery calls have Malik listening 70% of the time.

**Situation questions:**
1. "What's driving the interest in AI architecture right now? What changed?"
2. "Walk me through what you currently have in place. Models, systems, team."
3. "Who owns AI in your organization? Is there a clear architectural owner?"

**Problem questions:**
4. "When your AI system is wrong — makes a bad prediction, produces a bad output — what happens next?"
5. "If I asked you to explain the feedback loop in your current AI architecture, what would you say?"
6. "Where's the biggest gap between where you are and where you need to be?"

**Implication questions:**
7. "What's the cost of that gap? Not in dollars necessarily — in missed capability, in manual work, in lost time."
8. "What happens if the gap is still there in 18 months?"

**Decision questions:**
9. "Who else is involved in a decision like this?"
10. "What have you tried? What didn't work and why?"

## OBJECTION HANDLING GUIDE

### Objection 1: "This seems expensive for a 30-day audit."

**The wrong response:** "Well, let me explain why it's worth it..."

**The right response:**

"Let me ask you something first. What's the current monthly cost of the gap we were discussing? Not just the AI spend — the labor that's compensating for automation that doesn't exist yet, the decision latency from not having closed-loop sensing, the engineers maintaining a system that isn't improving."

*Pause and let them calculate.*

"The audit is $[X]. Most organizations I work with find that the gap they're operating with is costing them more than that every month — they just haven't measured it. The audit produces that measurement, among other things."

"If the measurement shows the cost is lower than you thought, the roadmap will still be valuable. If it shows what I suspect, the investment in the audit will be one of the more obvious decisions you've made this year."

---

### Objection 2: "We can probably figure this out internally."

**The wrong response:** Argue against it.

**The right response:**

"You might be right. Let me ask: who internally would own the architectural review? And do they have a framework for assessing closed-loop integrity and feedback loop design across your current stack?"

*Listen to the answer.*

"What I find is that internal teams usually have strong competency in the specific systems they've built, but they're missing the comparative framework — what Level 4 looks like vs. Level 6, where the Fitness Equation denominator is most likely growing, what it takes to move from a reactive system to an adaptive one. That's not a critique of your team. It's a structural gap in most engineering organizations."

"The audit doesn't replace your internal team. It gives them a framework to organize against and a prioritization of what to work on first."

---

### Objection 3: "We're not ready / we need to get some things in order first."

**The wrong response:** Push for urgency.

**The right response:**

"That's useful to understand. What specifically isn't in order?"

*Listen carefully. This often reveals the real objection — budget, internal politics, a competing initiative, or genuine timing.*

"In my experience, organizations that wait until they're 'ready' often find that 'ready' moves 90 days out every 90 days. The audit is designed precisely for organizations that aren't sure what 'ready' means in this context — it produces the diagnosis that defines what you'd actually need to prepare."

"What would need to be true for the timing to make sense in the next 60 days?"

---

### Objection 4: "We just went through an AI strategy exercise with [large consulting firm]."

**The wrong response:** Disparage the other firm.

**The right response:**

"How did that go? What did you get at the end?"

*Listen.*

"Strategy presentations are useful for alignment. What they often don't produce is an architectural diagnosis — a precise answer to 'where exactly is the closed-loop broken and why.' That's what the audit does."

"I'd actually suggest that if you have an existing strategy, the audit is easier — we can measure your current state against your stated strategy direction and identify the specific gaps between where your architecture is now and where the strategy assumes it will be."
